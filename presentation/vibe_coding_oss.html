Vibe Coding Kills Open Source?

Central question: Does the widespread adoption of vibe coding — AI-mediated software development — threaten the long-run sustainability of the open source software (OSS) ecosystem?

Motivation

  • AI coding assistants (Claude Code, Cursor, Lovable) are rapidly transforming how software is built
  • Vibe coding: a human translates intent into software via an AI agent, often without reading documentation or engaging with maintainers
  • OSS is nonrival and creates enormous social value — but many maintainers sustain projects through direct user engagement (documentation visits, bug reports, reputation)
  • If AI mediation substitutes for direct interaction, the same technology that makes software easier to use may erode the revenue base that motivates supply
  • No prior general-equilibrium analysis of how vibe coding feeds back into OSS entry and sharing decisions

Three Key Findings

  1. In the short run, when AI primarily boosts developer productivity, vibe coding raises OSS entry, quality, and welfare — while the mass of shared packages stays constant
  2. In the long run, under traditional engagement-based business models, vibe coding reduces OSS entry, variety, quality, and welfare — despite the productivity gains
  3. Sustaining OSS at its current scale requires per-user monetization to remain close to current levels, or a major shift toward platform redistribution, foundation grants, or enterprise funding

Background

What Is Vibe Coding?

  • Traditional developer workflow: select packages → read documentation → interact with maintainers → report bugs
  • Vibe coding: an AI agent selects, composes, and modifies packages end-to-end; the human developer may not know which upstream components were used

Two economic consequences:

  1. Productivity channel (positive): lowers the cost of using and building on existing code
  2. Demand-diversion channel (negative): reduces direct engagement → maintainers earn less private return per unit of usage

Why does this matter for OSS supply?

OSS maintainers typically capture value through engagement-based channels: documentation traffic generates consulting leads, GitHub stars create job opportunities, public Q&A signals demand. An AI intermediary that bypasses these surfaces can decouple usage from monetizable engagement.

AI Adoption: A Large, Fast Shock

Rapid diffusion across the industry

  • By October 2024, >25% of new code at Google was AI-generated and accepted by engineers (Pichai, 2024)
  • At Anthropic, CEO Dario Amodei reported in September 2025 that “70, 80, 90 percent of the code is written by Claude”
  • Using GitHub commit data from 170,000 developers, Daniotti et al. (2025) estimate AI generated ~29–30% of Python functions authored by U.S. contributors by end 2024

. . .

Capability improvements are equally striking

Year Model SWE-bench solve rate
2024 Claude 2 1.96%
Early 2026 Claude 4.5 74.2%

Rapid performance gains help explain why adoption accelerates sharply once capability crosses a usability threshold.

The Engagement Substitution Problem

Evidence that AI mediation decouples usage from engagement

Stack Overflow:

  • del Rio-Chanona et al. (2024) show ChatGPT access reduced Stack Overflow activity by ~25% within six months
  • Overall Stack Overflow traffic has continued declining sharply since (Orosz, 2025)

. . .

Tailwind CSS (a concrete case):

  • Weekly npm downloads: rising steadily — growing usage
  • Stack Overflow questions tagged tailwind-css: falling
  • Documentation traffic: down ~40% from early 2023 (creator Adam Wathan, 2026)
  • Revenue: down ~80% (Wathan, 2026)

. . .

Usage and engagement have decoupled — exactly the mechanism the model formalises.

Concentration in OSS Outcomes

OSS outcomes follow a Pareto distribution

  • Of ~125 million GitHub repositories, only 10.32 million have ever received at least one star
  • Among JavaScript npm packages: 840,419 published; only 392,828 ever appear as a dependency
  • Log–rank regressions on GitHub stars yield Pareto exponent slopes of 1.08–2.24 depending on the tail truncation

. . .

Why this matters for the model:

  • Most repositories attract no attention and are never reused
  • A small upper tail accounts for a large share of usage and downstream dependence
  • The model captures this with a Pareto quality distribution and an endogenous sharing cutoff

Model

Model Overview

A monopolistic competition framework for OSS

Inspired by Krugman (1980) and Melitz (2003), the model has four components:

  1. Users choose among packages that differ in quality using a Fréchet discrete-choice demand system — they value variety and quality
  2. Developers incur an up-front cost, draw project quality from a Pareto distribution, then decide whether to release as OSS by paying a fixed sharing cost
  3. Software production function: development cost decreases in existing software quality and variety — a “software-begets-software” feedback
  4. Vibe coding: adds an inner nest — after choosing a package, the user decides between direct and AI-mediated usage, affecting both utility and monetizable engagement

Users and Package Choice

Fréchet discrete-choice demand

A user’s utility from package \(k\) with quality \(q_k\):

\[U_{ik} = \nu_{ik} q_k u, \quad \nu_{ik} \sim \text{Fréchet}(\sigma, \cdot)\]

  • \(\sigma > 1\): substitutability across packages (higher = easier to switch)
  • \(u\): productivity multiplier (affected by vibe coding)

Expected user utility across the best available package:

\[U = \bar{q} \, u \, m_s^{1/\sigma}\]

where \(\bar{q}\) = average quality, \(m_s\) = mass of shared packages. Welfare rises with quality, productivity, and variety.

Developers and the Sharing Decision

Endogenous selection into sharing

  1. Developers pay up-front cost \(\Phi\) before quality is known
  2. After observing realized quality \(q \sim \text{Pareto}(\gamma)\), share if payoff exceeds cost:

\[\Pi(q) = \pi \cdot \frac{q^\sigma}{m_s \bar{q}^\sigma} \geq \tau\]

  • \(\pi\): reward per direct user (the key variable affected by vibe coding)
  • \(\tau\): fixed sharing cost (packaging, documentation, maintenance)

. . .

Equilibrium sharing cutoff \(q_0\): only projects with \(q \geq q_0\) are released as OSS.

\[m_s = \frac{\pi}{\tau \Lambda^\sigma}, \quad \bar{q} = \Lambda^{1+\sigma/\gamma} \left(\frac{\tau m}{\pi}\right)^{1/\gamma}\]

Notably, the mass of shared software \(m_s\) increases in the reward per user \(\pi\) — this is the channel vibe coding threatens.

Vibe Coding in the Model

An inner nest: how users interact with a chosen package

After selecting package \(k\), users choose between:

  • Direct usage: read docs, visit project page, discover commercial offerings → monetizable engagement
  • Vibe-coded usage: interact through an AI assistant trained on documentation → no direct engagement

The vibe coding adoption share follows an S-curve:

\[v = \frac{\zeta^\theta}{1 + \zeta^\theta}\]

where \(\zeta\) = AI capability (productivity of vibe coding) and \(\theta\) = substitutability between usage modes.

. . .

The utility multiplier from having the option: \(u = (1-v)^{-1/\theta} > 1\)

When \(\theta\) is large (modes are close substitutes), the productivity gain is modest even for high adoption — and the demand-diversion channel dominates more strongly.

Results

Short-Run Effects: Developer Adoption Only

When AI helps developers build, but users still engage directly

Under this scenario, \(\pi\) remains unchanged (users still interact directly with OSS):

Outcome Change Direction
Developer entry \(m\) \((1-v)^{-\beta/(\theta\eta)}\) Up
Shared packages \(m_s\) Unchanged Neutral
Average quality \(\bar{q}\) \((1-v)^{-\beta/(\theta\gamma\eta)}\) Up
User welfare \(U\) \((1-v)^{-\beta/(\theta\gamma\eta)}\) Up

. . .

  • More entry, higher quality — but the fraction of projects that are shared falls (entry rises, \(m_s\) constant → more unshared “AI slop”)
  • Consistent with reports that 20% of curl security reports in 2025 were AI-generated fabrications (Stenberg, 2025)

Long-Run Effects: Traditional Business Models

When engagement-based monetization erodes: \(\pi = \bar{\pi}(1-v)\)

Outcome Change Direction
Developer entry \(m\) \((1-v)^{1+\beta(1/\sigma - 1/\theta)/\eta}\) Down
Shared packages \(m_s\) \((1-v)\) Down
Average quality \(\bar{q}\) \((1-v)^{\beta(1/\sigma - 1/\theta)/(\gamma\eta)}\) Down
User welfare \(U\) \((1-v)^{1/\sigma - 1/\theta + \beta(1/\sigma - 1/\theta)/(\gamma\eta)}\) Down

where \(\eta = 1 - \beta/\gamma > 0\) and \(\theta > \sigma\) (usage modes are closer substitutes than packages).

. . .

The reward channel dominates: a 70% drop in vibe coding adoption (\(v = 0.7\)) causes only a ~12% reduction in development cost, but per-user revenue collapses by 70% under the traditional model.

The Horse Race: Which Channel Wins?

Two forces with opposite signs

Cost channel (positive): vibe coding lowers development costs

  • Elasticity of entry to productivity: \(\beta/\eta < 1\)
  • When \(\theta\) is large: productivity gain \(u = (1-v)^{-1/\theta}\) is small

. . .

Reward channel (negative): vibe coding erodes engagement-based revenue

  • Elasticity of entry to reward: \((1 + \beta/\sigma - \beta/\gamma)/\eta > 1\)
  • Amplified by the software-begets-software feedback: lower entry → worse ecosystem → higher development costs → even less entry

. . .

The same magnification mechanism that once accelerated OSS growth now accelerates contraction.

Minimum Monetization Bound

How much can per-user revenue fall before OSS collapses?

Sustaining baseline entry (\(m \geq m_0\)) requires:

\[\frac{\pi}{\pi_0} \geq (1-v)^\omega, \quad \omega \equiv \frac{1/\theta}{1/\beta + 1/\sigma - 1/\gamma} \in (0,1)\]

Quantitative example (\(v = 0.7\), \(\theta = 3\), calibrated \(\omega \approx 0.1\)):

  • Productivity benefit: development cost falls by ~12%
  • Maximum tolerable revenue decline: ~11% (i.e., \(\pi/\pi_0 \geq 0.89\))
  • Revenue decline under traditional model: ~70% (i.e., \(\pi/\pi_0 = 0.3\))

. . .

The gap between a 70% revenue collapse and an 11% sustainable decline is the central quantitative result.

Alternative Business Models

Two polar cases from a revenue decomposition

Let \(\alpha\) = share of revenue independent of usage mode; \(\rho\) = vibe discount (fraction of lost monetization for vibe-coded users).

Corollary 4 — Minimum vibe-coder contribution:

If all revenue is usage-dependent (\(\alpha = 0\)), sustaining entry requires:

\[\rho \leq \frac{1-(1-v)^\omega}{v}\]

For \(v = 0.7\), \(\omega = 0.1\): vibe coders must contribute at least 84% of what direct users contribute.

. . .

Corollary 5 — Minimum usage-independent revenue:

If vibe coders contribute nothing (\(\rho = 1\)), at least 84% of revenue must come from enterprise licensing or foundation grants.

Calibration

Parameter values and their empirical basis

Parameter Role Calibration target Value
\(\beta\) Software share in production Production-function evidence (ICT firms) 1/3
\(\gamma\) Quality tail thickness Pareto tail of GitHub stars 3
\(\sigma\) Package substitutability Video game demand elasticity (Clements & Ohashi, 2005) ≈1.5
\(\theta\) Usage-mode substitutability Field experiments on AI coding productivity 3–4
\(\zeta\) AI capability Implied by \((v, \theta)\) and observed adoption —

. . .

The asymmetry of elasticities — entry responds about 3.3× more strongly to monetization than to productivity — underlies the main result.

Discussion

Underprovision Is Already a Feature, Not a Bug

The baseline market already underprovides OSS

  • Developers capture a small fraction of the social value they create (empirical ratio: development cost ≈ 0.1% of user utility)
  • Free entry ensures zero expected profit — developers are indifferent about entering
  • A social planner would set \(m > m_{eq}\) because the private return understates the social marginal benefit

. . .

Vibe coding makes this pre-existing underprovision problem worse — not better — under traditional monetization, despite the productivity gains.

Policy Directions

Three mechanisms to close the private-social return gap

  1. “Spotify for OSS” — platform-level revenue redistribution
    • AI platforms already meter usage at fine granularity (tokens, tool calls, package downloads)
    • Revenue sharing based on attributable usage is technically feasible with existing infrastructure
    • Would reduce the vibe discount \(\rho\) toward zero
  2. Direct transfers to OSS infrastructure
    • Foundation grants, corporate sponsorships, government funding
    • GitHub Sponsors, npm funding URLs already exist as mechanisms
    • 2024 Open Source Software Funding Survey: $7.7B annually contributed — but concentrated in employee labor
  3. Alternative monetization channels
    • Enterprise licensing, API fees, developer-focused services
    • Partial but incomplete insurance: reduces rate of decline but cannot prevent collapse when \(\rho = 1\)

Limitations

  1. Abstracts from team collaboration — larger teams may mitigate negative effects, but the model treats each project as a single developer
  2. Nested logit assumes substitutability — the model assumes direct and vibe-coded usage are substitutes in utility; AI may also overlook parts of large packages due to context window limits
  3. Engagement-based monetization broadly defined — the paper treats reputation and job opportunities as engagement-dependent, which requires a broad interpretation
  4. No distinct enterprise/SaaS sector — large enterprises (>69% of the OSS services market) can price access directly, which changes how AI mediation affects their revenues
  5. Symmetric packages — in practice, infrastructure packages (high downstream dependence, low visibility) and application packages differ markedly in their exposure to vibe coding
  6. Key elasticities unquantified — \(\theta\) (usage-mode substitutability) and \(\beta\) (software-begets-software parameter) need empirical estimation; the paper’s results are sensitive to their magnitudes

Key Takeaways

  1. Vibe coding is already large and fast: AI tools went from negligible to majority usage within two years, driven by rapid capability improvements
  2. The short-run effect is positive: AI primarily boosts developer productivity, raising quality and welfare without eroding monetization
  3. The long-run effect is negative under traditional OSS business models: engagement-based revenue collapses faster than development costs fall
  4. The magnification mechanism runs in reverse: the same feedback loops that accelerated OSS growth will accelerate contraction
  5. Sustainable OSS requires new monetization institutions: platform redistribution, foundation grants, or enterprise funding must compensate for lost engagement revenue
  6. The model is a call to action, not a doom prophecy: the infrastructure for solutions largely exists — what is needed is coordination and will
